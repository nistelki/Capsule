Shared Patient Summary
Without Creating Liability

A working prototype and information architecture for a shared clinical record that is genuinely useful, clinically safe, and legally survivable — designed from a compliance-first perspective.

The problem
Multidisciplinary clinics share patients but not context — causing conflicting advice, missed flags, and patients as the information bridge.
The risk
Shared records create defensive documentation, liability exposure, and sensitive information leakage — often making care worse.
The approach
Three-tier permission model. Clinicians author directly to shared fields — nothing auto-extracted from private notes.
Background
Built by a compliance professional — treating governance, liability, and information architecture as the product design problem, not an afterthought.
How to use this prototype
1
Use the role switcher (top right) to change perspective — GP, Physio, Dietitian, Psychologist, or Admin. The summary transforms in real-time.
2
Navigate the seven sections in the left sidebar — Overview, Medications, Conditions, Timeline, Permission Model, Data Architecture, and Anti-CYA Design.
3
Watch what redacts. Psychology content, sensitive social history, and prescribing details each have different visibility rules by role.

Permission Model
Who sees what, and why
Three-Tier Visibility Structure

The permission model is built on three concentric tiers. Each tier is opt-in by default for sensitive categories, and clinically scoped — visibility follows need-to-know, not institutional convenience.

TIER 1 — Universal Safety
Visible to ALL roles including admin. No override.

· Drug allergies
· Critical safety flags
· Emergency contacts
· DNR / advance directive status
TIER 2 — Clinical Shared
Visible to all treating clinicians. Not admin.

· Active diagnoses
· Medications
· Functional status
· Coordination flags
· Visit outcomes
TIER 3 — Role-Scoped
Controlled by the originating clinician. Opt-in per field.

· Psych session flags (psychologist controls)
· Substance use history
· Sensitive social context
· Therapeutic rationale

Who Controls What

The originating clinician controls their tier-3 fields. A psychologist decides which flags cross into the shared record. A GP controls whether substance use history is shared beyond clinical roles. Patients can review what is shared and flag concerns — but cannot unilaterally remove safety-critical information.

Patients are shown a plain-language version of their summary at each visit. They cannot see the permission matrix or who has accessed their record in real-time, but receive a quarterly access log on request.

Data Architecture
What is summarised, what is excluded, and why
Core Principle: Summarise, Don't Aggregate

The shared summary is not a merged record — it is a structured set of clinician-authored summaries that are explicitly published to the shared space. Each clinician writes directly to the shared view; nothing is automatically extracted from their private notes.

This is the key architectural decision. It prevents raw clinical notes (with their defensive hedging, speculation, and therapeutic reasoning) from contaminating the shared space — while ensuring what does appear is intentional and clinically actionable.

Included vs Excluded
Category Included Excluded Rationale
Safety Allergies, drug interactions, risk flags, emergency info Context for how risk was assessed Safety first; clinicians need the flag, not the reasoning chain
Diagnoses Active, confirmed diagnoses with brief clinical status Differential diagnoses, ruled-out conditions, speculative notes Differentials create noise and false clinical signals across teams
Medications Name, dose, frequency, indication, prescriber Prescribing rationale, alternatives considered, patient refusals Actionable data only; rationale lives in GP notes
Psychology Clinician-authored flags: affect on treatment, safety status, engagement Session content, therapeutic disclosures, formulation, history Confidentiality is non-negotiable; flagging is a clinical decision
Social History Housing, work, support — relevant functional context Family conflict detail, past trauma, legal history Functional context improves care; sensitive details do not add clinical value across all roles
Visit Notes Encounter outcome, clinical decisions, coordination actions Session verbatim, patient statements, clinical reasoning drafts Outcomes are shared facts; reasoning is private professional judgment
Lab / Imaging Current values, trends, and ordering clinician's interpretation Raw reports, historical values beyond 12 months unless relevant Interpreted results, not data dumps
Patient Voice Patient-stated goals, patient-reported outcomes (PROs), stated preferences Verbatim patient statements Goals aid coordination; verbatim statements create confidentiality issues
Update Governance

Who can write: Each clinician writes only to their own section. Cross-discipline coordination fields require the initiating clinician to tag a specific colleague.

Staleness: Fields older than 90 days are visually flagged as potentially stale. Clinicians are prompted to confirm or archive. Safety flags never expire automatically.

Audit: Every field write and every view access is logged with timestamp and role. Not visible to other clinicians in real-time — available to the practice on request for governance review.

Anti-CYA Design
How Capsule prevents defensive, performative documentation
The Problem: Shared Records Create Defensive Writing

When clinicians know their notes will be read by colleagues — or might appear in litigation — they write for audiences other than the patient's care. The result is notes full of "patient was advised of risks," vague qualifications, and omitted concerns that make the writer look good but the care worse.

Capsule's architecture addresses this at the structural level, not through instruction.

  • 1
    Private notes stay private. Clinicians document freely in their own records. Only curated, intentional fields enter the shared summary. This separation removes the audience problem — private notes are written for care, not defence.
  • 2
    Structured fields, not free text. The shared summary uses defined fields (diagnoses, flags, coordination notes) rather than open-ended note space. It's harder to pad a structured field. "Active: Yes/No + one-line status" resists CYA inflation.
  • 3
    The shared record is explicitly not the legal record. Capsule's terms and UI make clear the shared summary is a care coordination tool. The medicolegal record lives in each clinician's private notes. This removes the incentive to use the summary as a paper trail.
  • 4
    Write for the next clinician, not the next lawyer. The UI prompt when adding shared content is: "What does the next treating clinician need to know?" Not: "Document your clinical decision." This shapes what gets written.
  • 5
    Audit without surveillance. Access logs exist for governance, not peer surveillance. Clinicians are not shown who has read their shared entries in real-time. This reduces the pressure to write defensively for an imagined audience of colleagues.
  • 6
    Coordination notes are ephemeral. Cross-discipline coordination entries have a 30-day default expiry and are labelled as working communication, not permanent documentation. This makes them more honest and less formal.

CYA vs Capsule: A comparison

⚠ Traditional Shared Record (CYA)
"Patient was counselled at length regarding risks of continued sedentary behaviour and the importance of adherence to the physiotherapy programme. Patient verbally acknowledged. Referral to dietitian completed and documented. Further review recommended."
✓ Capsule shared summary
"Stage 2 physio underway. Avoidance behaviours limiting evening exercise — physio and psych coordinating graded exposure. Dietitian to align exercise-nutrition timing. Next milestone: Stage 3 readiness by Apr."
The Capsule version tells the next clinician what is happening and what to do. The CYA version tells a judge that the clinician did their job.
